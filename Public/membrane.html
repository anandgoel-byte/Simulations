Conceptual Assessments

Question 1

How does Fick's First Law mathematically govern simple diffusion across cellular membranes?

Fick's First Law defines molecular solute flux ($J$) proportional to the concentration gradient: $$J = -P (C_{\text{in}} - C_{\text{out}})$$ Where $P$ is the membrane permeability coefficient: $$P = \frac{K_p D}{x}$$ ($K_p$: partition coefficient, $D$: diffusion constant, $x$: bilayer thickness). Nonpolar gases hold huge $K_p$, yielding swift spontaneous diffusion.
Question 2

What are the kinetic differences between simple diffusion and channel-facilitated transport?

Simple diffusion is non-saturable, showing a linear flux increase as concentration rises. Facilitated transport relies on channel saturation, conforming to Michaelis-Menten kinetics: $$J = \frac{J_{\text{max}} [S]}{K_M + [S]}$$ When solute concentration $[S] \gg K_M$, carrier bindings are completely saturated, reaching the asymptotic flux limit $J_{\text{max}}$.
Question 3

What is the stoichiometric ratio of ion exchange in active Sodium-Potassium pumps?

The $\text{Na}^+/\text{K}^+$-ATPase pump operates electogenically. Per single ATP hydrolyzed cycle, it pumps: $$\text{Stoichiometry: } 3\text{Na}^+ \text{ (outward)} : 2\text{K}^+ \text{ (inward)}$$ This active transport creates a net positive charge export, establishing cellular resting membrane potential ($\Delta\Psi \approx -70\text{ mV}$).
Question 4

How does the Nernst Equation compute the equilibrium potential for ionic species?

For a given ion of valence $z$, equilibrium potential ($E_{\text{eq}}$) balances the thermodynamic chemical gradient with electrical attraction: $$E_{\text{eq}} = \frac{RT}{zF} \ln \frac{[C]_{\text{outside}}}{[C]_{\text{inside}}}$$ At $37^\circ\text{C}$, the simplified coefficient for monovalent cations ($z = +1$) is: $$E_{\text{eq}} = 61.5 \log_{10} \frac{[C]_{\text{out}}}{[C]_{\text{in}}} \text{ mV}$$
Question 5

Why does the hydrophobic lipid core restrict polar solutes while permitting nonpolar ones?

The interior of the bilayer consists of fatty acid hydrocarbon tails. Spontaneous crossing requires solutes to shed their hydration shell: $$\Delta G_{\text{hydration}} \gg 0$$ Charged ions ($Na^+$) or highly polar molecules ($Glu$) face a massive thermodynamic barrier to desolvate and dissolve in the nonpolar core, requiring specialized hydrophilic protein conduits.

📘 CELLULAR MEMBRANE TRANSPORT - User Guide

Welcome to the Membrane Transport Virtual Lab! Analyze how molecules and ions traverse the selectively permeable cellular lipid bilayer.

🎯 Simulation Modes

  • Simple Diffusion: Focuses on spontaneous crossing of small, nonpolar molecules ($O_2, CO_2$) down concentration gradients without protein assistance.
  • Facilitated: Solutes require specific channel proteins. Toggle the Glucose channel Open or Closed to analyze saturable carrier transport.
  • Active Transport: Uses metabolic energy (ATP) to pump ions ($Na^+, K^+$) against concentration gradients via electogenic cycles.
  • Playground Sandbox: Combines all mechanics. Experiment freely by adding solute species or modifying thermal levels.

🔍 Operational Controls

  • Temperature Slider: Syncs with system kinetic energy to control particle velocities.
  • Concentration Dashboard: Provides a live count of particles Inside vs Outside the cytoplasm.
