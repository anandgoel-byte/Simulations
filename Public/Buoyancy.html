Conceptual Assessments

Question 1

State Archimedes' Principle and write its mathematical form.

Any object immersed in a fluid experiences an upward buoyant force equal to the weight of fluid displaced: $$F_b = \rho_F \cdot V_{sub} \cdot g$$ where $\rho_F$ is fluid density, $V_{sub}$ is the submerged volume, and $g$ is gravitational acceleration.
Question 2

Under what condition does an object float, sink, or achieve neutral buoyancy?

Buoyancy states depend on the ratio of object density ($\rho_o$) to fluid density ($\rho_F$): $$\rho_o < \rho_F \implies \text{Floats } (F_b > W)$$ $$\rho_o = \rho_F \implies \text{Neutral } (F_b = W)$$ $$\rho_o > \rho_F \implies \text{Sinks } (F_b < W)$$
Question 3

What fraction of a floating object's volume is submerged?

At vertical static equilibrium, upward Buoyant force exactly balances gravity Weight: $$W = F_b \implies \rho_o V g = \rho_F V_{sub} g$$ Canceling gravity $g$ yields the submerged fraction equation: $$\frac{V_{sub}}{V} = \frac{\rho_o}{\rho_F}$$
Question 4

How does fluid viscosity affect buoyancy and terminal velocity?

Viscosity does NOT affect buoyant force $F_b$. However, viscosity alters drag: $$F_{drag} = 6\pi \eta r v$$ At sinking/rising terminal velocity, drag force perfectly balances the net force difference: $$F_{drag} + F_b = W$$
Question 5

Why can a heavy steel ship (ρ ≈ 7.8 g/cm³) float easily on water?

A ship's hull is shaped to enclose a massive volume of air, making the effective average density much smaller: $$\rho_{eff} = \frac{M_{total}}{V_{total}} < \rho_{water}$$ Displacing water equal to its total massive weight keeps the ship floating.
