Question 1

How does Faraday's Law mathematically describe the induced electromotive force (EMF)?

Faraday's Law states that the induced electromotive force (EMF) $\mathcal{E}$ in a closed circuit is equal to the negative rate of change of the magnetic flux $\Phi_B$ through the circuit: $$\mathcal{E} = -N \frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$$ where $N$ is the number of loops. The magnetic flux $\Phi_B$ is modeled as: $$\Phi_B = \iint_{S} \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{A}$$
Question 2

What is the physical significance of the negative sign in Faraday's Law?

The negative sign represents **Lenz's Law**, reflecting the **Conservation of Energy**. The induced current creates a secondary magnetic field that opposes the original change in flux: $$\vec{\nabla} \times \vec{E} = -\frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t}$$ If the sign were positive, the field would reinforce the change, leading to infinite energy extraction.
Question 3

What is the mathematical formulation of motional EMF induced in a sliding conductor?

When a conductor of length $L$ travels at velocity $v$ perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field $B$, free charges experience a magnetic Lorentz force: $$\vec{F}_m = q(\vec{v} \times \vec{B})$$ This leads to charge separation and a steady state induced motional EMF: $$\mathcal{E} = \int (\vec{v} \times \vec{B}) \cdot d\vec{\ell} = v B L$$
Question 4

Why does the rotating generator wheel produce a sinusoidal Alternating Current (AC)?

As the magnet spins with angular frequency $\omega = 2\pi f$, the projection angle of the magnetic flux vector on the coil's normal varies as $\theta = \omega t$: $$\Phi_B(t) = B A \cos(\omega t)$$ Taking the derivative according to Faraday's law yields a sinusoidal AC electromotive output: $$\mathcal{E}(t) = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = B A \omega \sin(\omega t) = \mathcal{E}_0 \sin(\omega t)$$
Question 5

What are Eddy Currents and how do they demonstrate electromagnetic damping?

Eddy currents are closed loops of current induced within static bulk metal pieces subjected to changing magnetic environments. The induced currents experience a Lorentz resistive force: $$d\vec{F} = I(d\vec{\ell} \times \vec{B})$$ This force acts opposite to the direction of sliding motion, acting as a damping brake directly proportional to velocity.
