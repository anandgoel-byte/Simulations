🔬 Sigma (σ) vs. Pi (π) Bonding Mechanics

Understanding single and multiple bonds is impossible without overlapping shapes:

  • Sigma Bonds (σ): Formed by **head-on** overlapping along the internuclear axis. These have cylindrical symmetry and allow free rotation of atoms.
  • Pi Bonds (π): Formed by **sideways** overlapping of remaining unhybridized p-orbitals. These lock atomic rotation, enabling double/triple structures.

Question 1

What is the orbital hybridization of the central carbon atoms in Acetylene ($H-C\equiv C-H$)?

Linear combination mixing equations of $s$ and $p_z$ atomic wavefunctions: $$\psi_{\text{hybrid}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\psi_s \pm \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\psi_z$$ The remaining two perpendicular unhybridized $p$-orbitals ($p_x$, $p_y$) overlap laterally to form two $\pi$ bonds: $$\pi_x = p_x^A + p_x^B, \quad \pi_y = p_y^A + p_y^B$$
Question 2

Which of the following molecular geometries corresponds to $sp^3d^2$ hybridization of the central atom?

Octahedral geometries mix one $s$, three $p$, and two $d$ atomic orbitals together: $$\text{Linear Combination: } s + p_x + p_y + p_z + d_{z^2} + d_{x^2-y^2}$$ The resulting six hybrid orbitals are mutually orthogonal and point along the spatial Cartesian coordinates.
Question 3

What is the theoretical bond angle of methane ($CH_4$), which exhibits $sp^3$ hybridization?

By solving the spatial dot product of the hybrid orbital axes: $$\vec{\psi}_i \cdot \vec{\psi}_j = \cos\theta$$ For tetrahedral $sp^3$ hybrids, the coefficients dictate: $$\cos\theta = -\frac{1}{3} \implies \theta = \arccos\left(-\frac{1}{3}\right) \approx 109.47^\circ$$
Question 4

How are Pi ($\pi$) bonds formed in a double or triple bond?

Pi ($\pi$) bonds are characterized by a planar nodal surface passing through the internuclear axis: $$\psi_{\pi}(\vec{r}) = c_1 \phi_{2p_x}^A(\vec{r}) + c_2 \phi_{2p_x}^B(\vec{r})$$ Formed via sideways, lateral overlap of unhybridized $p$-orbitals perpendicular to the sigma bond axis.
