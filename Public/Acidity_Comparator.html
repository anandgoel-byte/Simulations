Conceptual Assessments

Question 1

Why is Phenol (pKₐ ~10) millions of times more acidic than Ethanol (pKₐ ~16)?

The acidity of a molecule depends on the stability of its conjugate base. In Ethanol, the negative charge on the Ethoxide ion is localized purely on the Oxygen atom (highly unstable). In Phenol, the Phenoxide ion's negative charge is delocalized via Resonance into the benzene ring, spreading the charge burden and greatly increasing stability.
Question 2

How does the -NO₂ group in p-Nitrophenol affect acidity?

The -NO₂ group is a strong Electron-Withdrawing Group (EWG). It pulls electron density away from the ring via inductive effects, and importantly, allows for an additional resonance structure where the negative charge resides on the highly electronegative Oxygen of the Nitro group itself. This heavily stabilizes the conjugate base, increasing acidity (pKₐ ~7.1).
Question 3

Why does the -CH₃ group in p-Cresol slightly decrease acidity compared to regular Phenol?

The Methyl (-CH₃) group is an Electron-Donating Group (EDG) via hyperconjugation and inductive effects. It pushes electron density into the ring, intensifying the negative charge of the phenoxide ion, thereby destabilizing it and slightly raising the pKₐ (~10.26).
Question 4

What physically happens to the C-O bond length after deprotonation in Phenol?

Because the lone pair of electrons on Oxygen delocalizes into the benzene ring, the C-O single bond gains partial double-bond character. Double bonds are shorter and stronger than single bonds, so the C-O bond length physically decreases.
