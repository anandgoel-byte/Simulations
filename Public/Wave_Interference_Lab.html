Conceptual Assessments

Question 1

How does path difference mathematically determine constructive and destructive interference?

Superposition of two waves $y_1 = A\cos(kr_1-\omega t)$ and $y_2 = A\cos(kr_2-\omega t - \phi)$ results in: $$y_{\text{net}} = 2A\cos\left(\frac{k\Delta L + \phi}{2}\right)\cos\left(k\frac{r_1+r_2}{2}-\omega t\right)$$ Constructive limit requires the path difference to be an integer multiple: $$\Delta L = m\lambda \quad (m = 0, 1, 2...)$$ Destructive limit aligns peak-to-trough: $$\Delta L = \left(m + \frac{1}{2}\right)\lambda$$
Question 2

In Active Noise Cancellation (ANC), how does the anti-noise phase shift suppress external ambient sound?

Incoming noise wave $P_n(t) = P_0\cos(\omega t)$. The ANC system plays an inverted anti-noise wave shifted by exactly $\phi = \pi$ rad ($180^\circ$): $$P_a(t) = P_0\cos(\omega t - \pi) = -P_0\cos(\omega t)$$ By linear algebraic superposition, the total acoustic pressure experienced is completely eliminated: $$P_{\text{net}}(t) = P_n(t) + P_a(t) = 0$$
Question 3

Under Huygens' Principle, how do coherent secondary slit sources propagate wavefronts?

Huygens' Principle states that every point on a primary wavefront acts as a source of secondary spherical wavelets of radius $r = v\Delta t$. The envelope of these wavelets defines the new wavefront: $$\Psi(\vec{r}, t) = \oint_{\text{aperture}} \frac{A}{s} e^{i(ks - \omega t)} dS$$ Thus, two narrow slits filter a plane wave into two perfectly synchronized, spherical sources that produce clean constructive and destructive patterns.
Question 4

What is the relation between wave frequency ($f$), wavelength ($\lambda$), and the velocity of propagation ($v$)?

Wavelength is the spatial distance between identical phases, and frequency is the rate of oscillation. The phase velocity $v$ is: $$v = \frac{\omega}{k}$$ Given $\omega = 2\pi f$ (angular frequency) and $k = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda}$ (wave number): $$v = \frac{2\pi f}{\frac{2\pi}{\lambda}} = f\lambda$$
Question 5

How does path difference vary as a function of the angle of propagation $\theta$ in double-slit setups?

Using the Fraunhofer parallel-ray approximation for a distant screen: $$\Delta L = r_2 - r_1$$ By drawing a perpendicular line from source $S_1$ to ray $S_2$, the right triangle with slit spacing $d$ and angle $\theta$ gives: $$\Delta L = d\sin\theta$$ For small angles, this maps to $\Delta L \approx d\tan\theta = d\frac{y}{D}$.

The Physics of Superposition

The **Principle of Superposition** states that when waves overlap, the net displacement at any point is the algebraic sum of the individual wave displacements: $$y_{\text{net}}(x, t) = y_1(x, t) + y_2(x, t)$$ This is the mechanical principle behind classical double-slit experiments first performed by Thomas Young in 1801, proving the wave nature of light.
