📋 Guided Quantum Atomic Laboratory Tasks

Follow these laboratory steps to interactively discover Bohr's predictions and Rydberg's spectroscopic constants.

Experiment 1

Deduce Rydberg's Constant ($R_H$) via Lyman Series

The Lyman series represents transitions of the electron in a hydrogen atom dropping from higher excited states to the ground state ($n_f = 1$).

1. Keep target species set to Hydrogen (Z=1).

2. Select orbit state n = 2 to excite, then click state n = 1 to decay.

3. Verify the emitted UV wavelength is exactly 121.6 nm. This is the Lyman-alpha line.

4. Decaying from n = 3 ➔ 1 yields Lyman-beta at 97.3 nm.

5. Calculate $R_H = \frac{1}{\lambda(1 - 1/n_i^2)}$ to see both converge to $\approx 1.097 \times 10^7\text{ m}^{-1}$.

Experiment 2

Identify the Visible Lines of the Balmer Series

Johann Balmer solved a mathematical relation for visible emission line wavelengths, which correspond to electron drops ending in the $n_f = 2$ orbit.

1. Perform the decay transition n = 3 ➔ 2. A visible red photon of wavelength 656.3 nm is logged.

2. Perform the decay n = 4 ➔ 2 to emit a turquoise photon of 486.1 nm.

3. Perform n = 5 ➔ 2 and n = 6 ➔ 2 to emit blue (434.0 nm) and violet (410.2 nm) lines.

4. Observe how these visible lines form Balmer's signature spectrograph fingerprint!

Experiment 3

Analyze Nuclear Scaling in Helium ($He^+$)

Hydrogen-like ions ($He^+$, $Li^{2+}$) have a single electron orbiting a nucleus with charge $Z$. This provides an ideal test for Bohr's postulate on the scaling of nuclear attraction forces.

1. Switch species to Helium⁺ (Z=2). Notice orbits shrink and energy levels scale by $Z^2 = 4$.

2. Ground state energy becomes -54.4 eV (four times Hydrogen's ground state $-13.6\text{ eV}$).

3. Set wave gun to 30.4 nm and click Inject Photon.

4. Verify the ground electron absorbs the extreme UV photon and jumps directly to n = 2.

📚 Bohr's Postulates & Atomic Equations

In 1913, Niels Bohr introduced a semi-classical model to explain the stability of atoms and the discrete line spectra of hydrogen. He bridged classical mechanics and early quantum concepts through three revolutionary postulates.

1. Quantized Angular Momentum Postulate

An electron revolves around the nucleus only in certain stable, non-radiating circular orbits called **stationary states**. Bohr postulated that the angular momentum ($L$) of an electron is quantized as integral multiples of $\hbar$:

$$L = m_e v_n r_n = n \hbar = \frac{n h}{2 \pi}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \dots$$

Where $m_e = 9.109 \times 10^{-31}\text{ kg}$ is the mass of the electron, $v_n$ is the speed on the $n$-th orbit, and $r_n$ is its radius.

2. Orbit Radiuses

By balancing Coulomb attraction force with classical centripetal force ($\frac{m_e v^2}{r} = \frac{Z e^2}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2}$), and substituting the momentum quantization condition, we solve for quantized radii:

$$r_n = \frac{n^2}{Z} \left( \frac{4 \pi \varepsilon_0 \hbar^2}{m_e e^2} \right) = a_0 \frac{n^2}{Z}$$

Where $a_0 \approx 0.529 \text{ Å}$ is the Bohr radius, the size of the ground-state Hydrogen atom.

3. Energy Levels of Hydrogen-like Atoms

The total mechanical energy of an electron in a quantized orbit (sum of kinetic and electrostatic potential energies) is given by:

$$E_n = -E_0 \frac{Z^2}{n^2} = -13.606 \text{ eV} \cdot \frac{Z^2}{n^2}$$

The negative sign indicates that the electron is bound to the nucleus. Ionization requires a positive energy of $-E_n$.

4. Orbital Transitions & Photon Wavelength

Radiation is emitted or absorbed only when the electron drops or rises between quantized states. The photon's wavelength ($\lambda$) is solved by the energy difference:

$$\Delta E = |E_i - E_f| = h f = \frac{h c}{\lambda} \implies \frac{1}{\lambda} = R_H Z^2 \left( \frac{1}{n_f^2} - \frac{1}{n_i^2} \right)$$

Question 1

What is Bohr's fundamental quantum condition for the stable stationary orbits of an electron?

Under Bohr's first postulate, orbital angular momentum is an integer multiple of Dirac's constant: $$L = m_e v r = n \hbar = \frac{n h}{2\pi}$$ This matches De Broglie's standing wave constraint on the orbital circumference: $$2\pi r = n \lambda \implies 2\pi r = n \frac{h}{p} \implies L = r p = n \frac{h}{2\pi}$$
Question 2

How does the radius of the Bohr orbits ($r_n$) scale with the principal quantum number $n$ and nuclear charge $Z$?

Coulomb force balancing centripetal acceleration yields: $$\frac{m_e v^2}{r} = \frac{Z e^2}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2}$$ Substituting $v = \frac{n \hbar}{m_e r}$ solves for the quantized radius: $$r_n = \left(\frac{4\pi\varepsilon_0 \hbar^2}{m_e e^2}\right) \frac{n^2}{Z} = a_0 \frac{n^2}{Z}$$
Question 3

What is the ground-state ionization potential of Singly Ionized Helium ($He^+$, $Z=2$)?

The orbital energy levels scale quadratically with the nuclear charge $Z$: $$E_n = -E_0 \frac{Z^2}{n^2} = -13.606 \text{ eV} \cdot \frac{Z^2}{n^2}$$ For Helium ($Z=2$) at ground state ($n=1$): $$E_1 = -13.606 \text{ eV} \cdot \frac{4}{1} = -54.42 \text{ eV}$$ The ionization potential is the positive magnitude: $+54.4\text{ eV}$.
Question 4

Which spectral series corresponds to transitions ending in the $n_f = 2$ shell, emitting visible light?

The Rydberg formula calculates the emission wavelengths: $$\frac{1}{\lambda} = R_H Z^2 \left( \frac{1}{n_f^2} - \frac{1}{n_i^2} \right)$$ For $n_f = 2$, transitions from $n_i = 3, 4, 5, 6$ yield wavelengths: $656.3\text{ nm}$ (red), $486.1\text{ nm}$ (cyan), $434.0\text{ nm}$ (blue), and $410.2\text{ nm}$ (violet), spanning the visible spectrum.
