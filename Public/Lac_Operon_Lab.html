NCERT Core Concept

Regulation of the lac operon by the repressor protein is negative regulation. The regulatory gene $i$ (inhibitor) constitutively produces the repressor, which blocks RNA polymerase. Lactose (the inducer) binds and inactivates the repressor to switch the operon ON.

📘 LAC OPERON NCERT PROTOCOL

This virtual laboratory models gene regulation in E. coli based strictly on the NCERT Class 12 Molecular Genetics curriculum.

🧬 The Operon DNA Components

  • i Gene (Regulator): Transcribes repressor mRNA, which is translated into repressor protein. It is active constitutively (all the time).
  • p (Promoter): Binding site for RNA Polymerase.
  • o (Operator): Physical binding site for the repressor protein.
  • Structural Genes (z, y, a): Transcribed into a single polycistronic lac-mRNA.
    • z (β-Galactosidase): Hydrolyzes lactose into Glucose & Galactose.
    • y (Permease): Multiplies membrane permeability to allow lactose entry.
    • a (Transacetylase): Transacetylase helper enzyme.

🔄 Inducer Action (Negative Control)

  • Absence of Lactose: The active repressor protein binds to the Operator, blocking RNA Polymerase from moving past promoter. Transcription = OFF.
  • Presence of Lactose (Inducer): Lactose binds to the repressor, changing its conformation and inactivating it. The repressor detaches, allowing RNA Polymerase to transcribe $z, y, a$.
  • Neet High-Yield Note: A baseline expression level of the operon is always required, as lactose cannot enter the cell without a few permease channels initially present!
