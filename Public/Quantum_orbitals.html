Conceptual Assessments

Question 1

Why do we see alternating red and blue rings in higher $s$ orbitals (like $3s$ or $4s$)?

These represent Radial Nodes. A radial node occurs when the radial component of the wavefunction $R_{n,l}(r)$ passes through zero and changes mathematical sign (phase). The dark black gaps in the canvas are the exact nodes where probability is zero.
Question 2

Why does the $p_z$ ($l=1, m_l=0$) orbital appear completely empty when viewing the XY plane?

The $p_z$ orbital is aligned strictly along the Z-axis. The entire XY plane ($z=0$) acts as an Angular Node for this orbital. Therefore, taking a 2D slice directly through the XY plane yields zero probability density. Switch to the XZ plane to see its lobes!
Question 3

What dictates the spatial orientation of an orbital?

The Magnetic Quantum Number ($m_l$). It determines the projection of angular momentum along a specified axis, essentially splitting a subshell (like $p$ or $d$) into specifically oriented individual orbitals. It ranges from $-l$ to $+l$.

📘 2D ORBITAL PROTOCOLS

Welcome to the 2D Quantum Number Virtual Laboratory! Explore the probability densities of atomic orbitals precisely mapped onto 2D slicing planes.

  • Principal (n): Defines the energy shell (Size).
  • Azimuthal (l): Defines the subshell shape (s, p, d, f). Note that $l$ is strictly capped at $n-1$.
  • Magnetic (m_l): Defines orientation. Restricted from $-l$ to $+l$.
  • Slicing Plane: If an orbital appears completely dark, you are likely viewing it cross-sectioned through one of its angular nodes! Change the Slicing Plane to reveal the lobes.
