Conceptual Assessments

Question 1

How do Maxwell's equations show that EM waves travel at the speed of light?

Taking curl of Faraday's law and substituting Ampere-Maxwell's law in vacuum gives the wave equation: $$\nabla^2 \vec{E} = \mu_0\epsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2\vec{E}}{\partial t^2}$$ Comparing this directly to the classical wave equation yields the wave phase velocity: $$v = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0\epsilon_0}} \approx 3.00 \times 10^8\text{ m/s} = c$$
Question 2

Why are $\vec{E}$ and $\vec{B}$ fields transverse and orthogonal to propagation?

Gauss's laws in vacuum dictate $\vec{\nabla}\cdot\vec{E}=0$ and $\vec{\nabla}\cdot\vec{B}=0$. For plane wave propagating along z: $$\frac{\partial E_z}{\partial z} = ik E_z = 0 \implies E_z = 0$$ $$\frac{\partial B_z}{\partial z} = ik B_z = 0 \implies B_z = 0$$ Thus, field vibrations have no parallel components along propagation axis.
Question 3

What is the physical definition of the Poynting Vector ($\vec{S}$)?

The Poynting vector represents the instantaneous directional energy flux density (watts per unit area): $$\vec{S} = \frac{1}{\mu_0}(\vec{E} \times \vec{B})$$ With $\vec{E} = E_y\hat{j}$ and $\vec{B} = B_x\hat{i}$, the vector cross product direction maps to: $$\vec{S} = -\frac{1}{\mu_0}E_yB_x\hat{k}$$ Because $B_x$ is in anti-phase relative to $E_y$, $S_z$ is always non-negative: $$S_z \propto \sin^2(kz-\omega t) \ge 0$$
Question 4

What is the exact ratio between the amplitudes of $\vec{E}$ and $\vec{B}$?

Applying plane wave equations to Faraday's induction curl $\vec{\nabla}\times\vec{E} = -\frac{\partial\vec{B}}{\partial t}$ gives: $$\frac{\partial E_y}{\partial z} = -\frac{\partial B_x}{\partial t} \implies k E_0 = \omega B_0$$ This simplifies directly to the vacuum velocity factor ratio: $$\frac{E_0}{B_0} = \frac{\omega}{k} = c$$
Question 5

How does radiation pressure ($P$) scale with Poynting energy flux?

Light waves transport physical momentum. The time-averaged radiation pressure $P$ on a perfectly absorbing surface is: $$P = \frac{\langle S \rangle}{c}$$ If the surface is perfectly reflecting, momentum transfer doubles: $$P = \frac{2\langle S \rangle}{c}$$
