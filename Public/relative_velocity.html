Conceptual Assessments

Question 1

Why does aiming the boat straight across ($\theta = 0^\circ$) result in drifting downstream?

Velocity is a vector. The boat's true velocity relative to Earth ($v_{b \to e}$) is the vector sum: $$\vec{v}_{b \to e} = \vec{v}_{b \to r} + \vec{v}_{r \to e}$$ Aiming straight provides zero horizontal counter-velocity. The river's flow dictates the entire horizontal component, pushing it downstream.
Question 2

What is the mathematical condition to cross the river with exactly ZERO drift?

To achieve zero drift, the net horizontal velocity must be zero. The boat must aim upstream to cancel the river's flow: $$v_{bx} + v_r = 0 \implies v_b \sin(\theta) + v_r = 0$$ Solving for the angle: $$\sin(\theta) = -\frac{v_r}{v_b}$$
Question 3

Under what condition is it IMPOSSIBLE to achieve a straight path (zero drift) across the river?

Based on $\sin(\theta) = -v_r / v_b$, the sine function cannot exceed a magnitude of 1. Therefore, if the river velocity is greater than the boat's maximum speed ($v_r > v_b$), it is physically impossible to cancel the drift. The boat will always be swept downstream.
Question 4

Which aiming angle provides the absolute MINIMUM time to cross the river?

Crossing time depends ONLY on the vertical component of velocity ($v_y$) and the river width ($W$): $$t = \frac{W}{v_y} = \frac{W}{v_b \cos(\theta)}$$ To minimize time, $\cos(\theta)$ must be maximized (value of 1). This occurs at $\theta = 0^\circ$ (aiming straight across), regardless of how far downstream you drift!
* Physics verified via 2D vector summation kinematics. Apparatus: River Width W = 100m
