📚 Matter Waves & Wave-Particle Duality

In 1924, French physicist Louis de Broglie made a bold proposal: if light waves exhibit particle properties (as Einstein proved with the photoelectric effect), then physical particles should also exhibit wave-like characteristics.

1. The de Broglie Relationship

The de Broglie wavelength ($\lambda$) of any moving matter is inversely proportional to its linear momentum ($p$):

$$\lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{m v}$$

Where $h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J}\cdot\text{s}$ is Planck's constant, $m$ is the mass in kg, and $v$ is the velocity in m/s.

2. Kinetic Energy Formulation

Using the non-relativistic relationship between momentum and kinetic energy ($K = \frac{p^2}{2m}$), we express the de Broglie wavelength as:

$$\lambda = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2mK}}$$

3. Quantum Double-Slit Diffraction

When a beam of matter waves hits two narrow slits separated by a distance $d$, waves diffracting from both slits interfere constructively or destructively on a detector screen. The distance between consecutive bright fringes ($\Delta y$) on a screen at distance $L$ is given by:

$$\Delta y = \frac{\lambda L}{d}$$

This confirms that a **smaller wavelength** (resulting from higher mass or velocity) yields **tighter, closely spaced interference fringes**.

Question 1

Under what condition does a baseball exhibit matter wave properties according to de Broglie?

The matter wavelength is given by $\lambda = \frac{h}{p}$. For macroscopic baseballs ($m = 0.145\text{ kg}$), momentum is huge relative to $h \approx 6.63 \times 10^{-34}\text{ J}\cdot\text{s}$, yielding $\lambda \approx 10^{-34}\text{ m}$. Wave properties only become observable if Planck's constant ($h$) is scaled up by a massive factor ($10^{30}$), or if momentum is infinitely small.
Question 2

What occurs to the spacing of the double-slit diffraction fringes if the velocity of a particle increases?

The double-slit fringe spacing $\Delta y$ is directly proportional to wavelength: $$\Delta y = \frac{\lambda L}{d}$$ Since wavelength is inversely proportional to velocity ($\lambda = \frac{h}{m v}$), increasing velocity decreases wavelength: $$\Delta y \propto \frac{1}{v}$$ Thus, faster-moving matter waves yield tighter, closely spaced diffraction peaks.
Question 3

Which historic experiment first directly confirmed the wavelike diffraction of moving electrons?

In 1927, Davisson and Germer fired slow-moving electrons at a crystalline Nickel target. The scattered electrons exhibited angular intensity peaks matching Bragg's diffraction law: $$n\lambda = 2d \sin\theta$$ This provided the first direct, empirical validation of de Broglie's matter wave hypothesis.
Question 4

How does Zeilinger's Buckyball ($C_{60}$) diffraction experiment illustrate the limits of wave-particle duality?

Zeilinger's team successfully diffracted $C_{60}$ fullerenes (comprising 60 carbon atoms with massive molecular weight $720$ amu). Despite their complexity and internal degrees of freedom, the coherent propagation of the Buckyball's center-of-mass wavefunction yielded clear quantum interference, verifying that wave-particle duality extends into the molecular boundary.
