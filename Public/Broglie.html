Conceptual Assessments

Question 1

Why don't we observe matter waves for everyday objects like baseballs?

Because Planck's constant ($h$) is incredibly small ($\approx 6.626 \times 10^{-34}$). Since macroscopic objects have massive momentum ($p=mv$), the resulting wavelength $\lambda = h/p$ is infinitesimally small (e.g., $10^{-34}$ meters), making diffraction impossible to observe.
Question 2

What happens to the double-slit fringes if particle velocity increases?

Fringe spacing $\Delta y$ is directly proportional to wavelength ($\Delta y = \lambda L / d$). If velocity increases, momentum increases, causing wavelength $\lambda$ to decrease. Therefore, the fringes compress and get closer together.
Question 3

What does the single-particle interference pattern prove?

It proves that a particle's probability wave interferes with *itself*. Even if fired one at a time, particles land as discrete dots, but over time, their distribution builds the exact interference pattern predicted by wave mechanics.
