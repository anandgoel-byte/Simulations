Ohm's Law & Electrical Resistance Theory

Ohm’s law states that at a constant physical temperature, the electric current ($I$) flowing through a metallic conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference ($V$) applied across its ends.

$$V \propto I \implies V = I \cdot R$$

1. Microscopic Origin of Resistance

Resistance arises due to the collision of drifting electrons with the vibrating positive metallic ions inside the crystal lattice. According to Drude-Lorentz model:

$$R = \left(\frac{m}{n e^2 \tau}\right) \frac{L}{A} \implies R = \rho \frac{L}{A}$$

Where fundamental parameters are defined as:

  • $m = 9.11 \times 10^{-31}\text{ kg}$ (Electron mass)
  • $n$ is the free electron density of the metal
  • $e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}\text{ C}$ (Electron charge)
  • $\tau$ is the average relaxation time between collisions
  • $\rho$ is the specific resistivity of the wire material

2. Geometrical Dependencies

Resistance scales directly with the length ($L$) of the wire and inversely with the cross-sectional area ($A = \pi \cdot (d/2)^2$):

$$R = \rho \frac{L}{\pi \left(\frac{d}{2}\right)^2} = \frac{4 \rho L}{\pi d^2}$$

Laboratory Experimental Procedure

Follow these precise board practical steps to verify relationships between current, voltage, and geometry:

1

Verify V vs I

Keep length, material (Copper), and diameter constant. Toggle the Key ON. Vary battery voltage from 0V to 20V. Click "Record Data Point" to register the readings into the observation log.

2

Analyze R vs Length

Fix voltage (e.g. 6.0 V), material, and diameter. Slide length from 10cm to 100cm. Notice how resistance climbs linearly while current drops, indicating direct proportionality ($R \propto L$).

3

Verify R vs Area

Fix length and voltage. Vary wire diameter from 0.1mm to 2.0mm. Record readings to establish that resistance is inversely proportional to diameter squared ($R \propto 1/d^2$).
