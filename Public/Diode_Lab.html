Conceptual Assessments

Question 1

Why does current increase exponentially during Forward Bias?

Applying a positive voltage to the P-type side opposes the built-in potential barrier. This drastically narrows the depletion region, lowering the energy hill. Because the energy distribution of charge carriers follows Maxwell-Boltzmann statistics, lowering the barrier allows an exponentially larger number of majority carriers to diffuse across the junction.
Question 2

Why does the Depletion Region widen during Reverse Bias?

In reverse bias, the positive battery terminal is connected to the N-side and the negative to the P-side. This pulls the free electrons and holes away from the junction. The removal of mobile carriers leaves behind uncovered, fixed ionic charges (donors/acceptors), physically widening the insulating depletion layer and increasing the barrier voltage.
Question 3

What causes the sudden massive current during Breakdown (e.g., at -10V)?

At high reverse voltages, the electric field across the depletion region becomes immense. In Zener Breakdown, the field is strong enough to physically rip electrons out of their covalent bonds via quantum tunneling. In Avalanche Breakdown, fast-moving minority carriers collide with atoms, knocking loose more electrons in a cascading chain reaction.
Question 4

Why does Germanium (Ge) conduct at a lower voltage than Silicon (Si)?

Germanium has a smaller bandgap energy ($\approx 0.67 \text{ eV}$) compared to Silicon ($\approx 1.1 \text{ eV}$). Because the electrons are less tightly bound to the nucleus in the larger Germanium atom, the built-in potential barrier is lower ($\approx 0.3 \text{V}$ vs $0.7 \text{V}$ for Si), requiring less forward voltage to overcome it.
