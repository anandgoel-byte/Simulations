Conceptual Assessments

Question 1

Why do electrons flow from the Anode to the Cathode?

Electrons flow spontaneously from the metal with the lower standard reduction potential (Anode) to the metal with the higher standard reduction potential (Cathode). The Cathode has a stronger "pull" on electrons.
Question 2

What is the critical function of the Salt Bridge?

As electrons leave the anode, positive ions build up. As electrons arrive at the cathode, negative ions build up. The salt bridge allows spectator ions (like K⁺ and NO₃⁻) to flow, neutralizing this charge buildup. Without it, the circuit would immediately break.
Question 3

What happens to the Voltage ($E_{cell}$) as the battery runs over time?

As the reaction proceeds, the Anode concentration increases (products) and the Cathode concentration decreases (reactants). According to the Nernst Equation, this increases $Q$, which mathematically decreases $E_{cell}$ until it reaches 0 (equilibrium/dead battery).
Question 4

Why does changing the Anode to Iron (Fe) drop the initial voltage compared to Zinc (Zn)?

Zinc ($E^\circ = -0.76\text{V}$) is a stronger reducing agent (more willing to give up electrons) than Iron ($E^\circ = -0.44\text{V}$). The larger the gap between the oxidation potential and the reduction potential, the higher the overall cell voltage.
